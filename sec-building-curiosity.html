    Section 2.1 Building Data Curiosity

    Data science begins with curiosity—wondering about the world and asking questions that can be explored through information. But not all questions are “data questions.” Let’s explore what makes a good question for data investigation.
    🔗

    Exploration 2.1.1. What Makes a Good Data Question?

    Consider these questions a student might ask. Which ones could be explored through collecting and analyzing information?
    🔗
    Question 1: “What’s the best color?”
    🔗
    This is an opinion question. While we could survey people about favorite colors, there’s no objective “best” color.
    🔗
    Question 2: “Do students in our school prefer summer or winter?”
    🔗
    This is a great data question! We could survey students and analyze the results to find patterns.
    🔗
    Question 3: “Why do people like music?”
    🔗
    This is too broad and philosophical. We might refine it to “What types of music are most popular among teenagers?”
    🔗
    Question 4: “Has the weather in our town changed over the past 10 years?”
    🔗
    Excellent data question! We could gather weather data and look for trends over time.
    🔗
    Notice the pattern? Good data questions are specific, measurable, and can be answered by collecting information.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.1.1. Teaching Tip: The Question Transformation Game.

    When students ask broad or opinion-based questions, don’t shut them down! Instead, play the “Question Transformation Game.” Help them reshape their curiosity into something investigable.
    🔗
    Student asks: “Why is math hard?”
    🔗
    You might transform it to: “What math topics do students in our class find most challenging?” or “How much time do students spend on math homework compared to other subjects?”
    🔗
    🔗

    Exploration 2.1.2. Try This Week: Curiosity Starters.

    Time needed: 10 minutes during any lesson
    🔗
    How it works: Use these sentence starters to help students generate data questions related to your current topic.
    🔗
    Elementary (K-5) Starters:
    🔗
    “I wonder how many...”
    🔗
    “Which one is more popular...”
    🔗
    “Do most kids in our class...”
    🔗
    “How often does...”
    🔗
    Secondary (6-12) Starters:
    🔗
    “What factors influence...”
    🔗
    “How has [topic] changed over time...”
    🔗
    “What’s the relationship between...”
    🔗
    “Do students who [X] also tend to [Y]...”
    🔗
    Example in action: During a lesson on weather (Science), a student uses “I wonder how many...” to ask “I wonder how many rainy days we have each month?” Perfect! Now you have a data investigation opportunity.
